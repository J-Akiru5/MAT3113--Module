Welcome, future math educators! This course is a departure from traditional, procedure-heavy mathematics. Here, we will not be given a problem and shown the method to solve it. Instead, we will become mathematicians—posing our own questions, exploring patterns, making conjectures, and building arguments.

**By the end of this module, you will be able to:**

1. Articulate a definition of problem solving.

2. Differentiate between routine and non-routine problems.

3. Outline the steps in problem solving from personal practice vis a vis those outlined by Polya and others.

4. Explain the significance of problem-solving in mathematics education.

- **PROBLEM SOLVING: DEFINITION AND PROCESS**

**What is Mathematical Investigation?**

**Routine Problem-Solving:** "Solve for x: 2x + 5 = 11." (One path, one correct answer).
**Mathematical Investigation:** "Explore the patterns and properties of the sequence generated by 2x + 5 for different integer values of x." (Multiple paths, multiple discoveries).

**Definition:** A *mathematical investigation *is an open-ended, systematic exploration of a mathematical situation. It is a **process-oriented** activity where the focus is on the journey of discovery: asking "what if?" questions, testing hypotheses, and formulating generalizations.

Problem solving is the process of applying mathematical knowledge, skills, and reasoning to find solutions to unfamiliar or non-routine situations where the solution path is not immediately apparent.

**Why is this crucial for you as a teacher?**

- **Fosters Critical Thinking **since it moves students beyond memorization.

- **Develops Perseverance **and encourages learning from "productive struggle.

- **Models Authentic Mathematics **by showing students what mathematicians actually *do*.

- **Promotes a Growth Mindset **because it emphasizes process over the "right answer.

**Polya's Four-Step Process **

**Polya's Four-Step Process** is a systematic method for solving problems, developed by the renowned mathematician George Pólya in his 1945 classic book, "How to Solve It." It is not a rigid formula but a flexible cycle of thinking that guides a problem-solver from confusion to solution.

The four steps are:

- **Understand the Problem**

- **Devise a Plan**

- **Carry Out the Plan**

- **Look Back**

**The ****Problem:**  A farmer has chickens and pigs. There are 50 heads and 140 legs. How many chickens and pigs does the farmer have?

**Step 1: Understand the Problem**

This is the most critical step. You cannot solve a problem you don't understand.

**What to do:**

- **Identify the Unknowns:** What are you trying to find?

*We need to find the number of chickens and the number of pigs.*

- **Identify the Knowns:** What information are you given?

*Total heads = 50. This means the total number of animals (chickens + pigs) is 50.*

*Total legs = 140.*

*We know a chicken has 2 legs and a pig has 4 legs.*

- **Restate the problem in your own words.**

*"We have two types of animals. Together, they have 50 heads and 140 legs. The chickens have 2 legs each, and the pigs have 4 legs each. We need to find out how many of each there are."*

- **Determine if there are any conditions or constraints.**

*The number of chickens and pigs must be whole numbers (you can't have half a chicken).*

*The number of each cannot be negative.*

**Key Question for this Step:** *"What is the problem asking for?"*

**Step 2: Devise a Plan**

This is where you choose a strategy or "heuristic" to connect the known information to the unknowns.

**What to do:**

- **Recall similar problems you have solved before.**

- **Consider different strategies.** Common strategies include:

- **Guess and Check:** Make a sensible guess and see if it works.

- **Make a Table:** Organize your guesses systematically in a table.

- **Draw a Diagram:** Could you draw a picture of the animals?

- **Use a Model:** Use blocks or bars to represent the animals.

- **Write an Equation:** This is an algebraic approach.

**Choosing a Plan for our problem:**

- Let's choose the **"Make a Table"** strategy. We can make educated guesses for the number of chickens and then calculate the number of pigs and the total legs.

**Key Question for this Step:** *"How will I connect the information I have to what I need to find?"*

**Step 3: Carry Out the Plan**

This is the execution step. You implement the strategy you chose in Step 2 carefully and patiently.

**Executing the "Make a Table" Plan:**

Let’s start by guessing 25 chickens (which means 25 pigs, since there are 50 heads).

| Chickens | Pigs | Chicken Legs (C x 2) | Pig Legs (P x 4) | Total Legs | Check (140?) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 25 | 25 | 50 | 100 | 150 | Too Many |
| 30 | 20 | 60 | 80 | 140 | **Correct!** |

We found the solution! If we have 30 chickens and 20 pigs:

- Heads: 30 + 20 = 50 ✓

- Legs: (30 × 2) + (20 × 4) = 60 + 80 = 140 ✓

**Key Question for this Step:** *"Am I implementing my plan correctly and checking my work as I go?"*

**Step 4: Look Back**

This step is about reflection and extension. It separates good problem-solvers from great ones.

**What to do:**

- **Check the solution:** Verify that the answer is correct. *(We did this above).*

- **Consider alternative solutions.** Could we have solved it another way?

**Algebraic Approach:**

Let c = number of chickens, and p = number of pigs.

Equation 1 (Heads): c + p = 50

Equation 2 (Legs): 2c + 4p = 140

Solve the system:

p = 50 – c  (From Equation 1).  Substitute into Equation 2.

 2c + 4(50 - c) = 140

2c + 200 - 4c  = 140

-2c = 140 – 200

-2c = -60

**                                      ****c = 30**** **

If c = 30, then if we substitute it into equation 1,  **p**** = 20.**

**Summarize each ****step**** using Polya’s Process**

- **Understand the Problem**

Known: 50 heads total, 140 legs total

Unknown: Number of chickens and pigs

Conditions: Chickens have 2 legs, pigs have 4 legs

- **Devise a Plan**

Strategy options: Make a table, use algebra, guess and check

- **Carry Out the Plan** (Using guess and check)

Try 25 chickens (50 legs) and 25 pigs (100 legs) = 150 legs (too many)

Try 30 chickens (60 legs) and 20 pigs (80 legs) = 140 legs ✓

- **Look Back**

Verify: 30 chickens + 20 pigs = 50 heads ✓, 60 legs + 80 legs = 140 legs ✓

Alternative: Could use algebraic equations

- **PROBLEM SOLVING AND MATHEMATICS EDUCATION**

**What is a "Problem" in Mathematics?**

**A Problem vs. An Exercise**

**Exercise: A routine task where the method of solution is immediately known**

Example: **Solve 2x + 5 = 15 (student knows the algebraic procedure)**

**Problem: A task where the solution method is not immediately apparent to the solve**

Example: **A farmer has chickens and pigs. There are 50 heads and 140 legs. How many of each animal does he have?" (requires thinking about strategy)**

**Why Problem Solving is Central to Mathematics Education?**

- Develops critical thinking and reasoning skills

- Promotes deeper conceptual understanding

- Prepares students for real-world applications

- Fosters mathematical creativity and perseverance

Problem-solving is the heart of mathematics (NCTM Standards, 1989).

**Sample Classroom Activity: "The Handshake Problem"**

- Situation: If 10 people each shake hands with everyone else, how many handshakes occur?

- Educational Value: Encourages multiple solution strategies (drawing, tables, patterns)

**1.3 PROBLEM SOLVING AND THE CONCEPTUAL FRAMEWORK OF THE K TO 12 MATHEMATICS CURRICULUM**

**Integration in K to 12 Curriculum:**

**Philosophical Foundation**

The K to 12 Mathematics Curriculum views mathematics as:

- A vehicle for developing critical thinking and problem-solving

- A tool for understanding and interacting with the world

- A language for expressing patterns and relationships

**Curriculum Components Emphasizing Problem Solving**

**Content Standards**

Focus on students' ability to:

- Understand mathematical concepts

- Apply mathematics to problem-solving situations

- Communicate mathematical thinking

**Performance Standards**

Expect students to:

- Formulate and solve problems

- Use appropriate strategies and tools

- Justify and explain solutions

**Spiral Progression Approach**

Problem-solving skills are developed across grade levels:

- **Grades 1-3:** Basic problem solving with whole numbers

- **Grades 4-6:** More complex problems with fractions, decimals, and basic geometry

- **Grades 7-10:** Algebraic thinking and more abstract problems

- **Senior High:** Applied problems in specialized contexts

**Sample Integration in K to 12**

**Grade 7 Example Competency:** Solves problems involving algebraic expressions and linear equations.

**Sample Problem:** Maria is 5 years older than Jose. The sum of their ages is 31. Find their ages.

**Teaching Approach:**

- Use bar models to represent the problem

- Develop algebraic equations: Let x = Jose's age, then x + (x+5) = 31

- Solve and verify the solution: 2x + 5 = 31 → 2x = 26 → x = 13 (Jose), x+5 = 18 (Maria)

- **REFLECTIONS ON RESEARCH STUDIES ON IMPLEMENTING PROBLEM SOLVING**

**Findings from Research:**

- Students in problem-centered classrooms show better conceptual understanding and retention

- Teachers need extensive professional development in problem-solving pedagogy

- Cultural factors influence implementation success

A 2019 study showed that Filipino students improved problem-solving performance when teachers used local context problems (e.g., market situations, transportation scenarios).

**Effective Implementation Strategies**

**For Classroom Practice**

- **Start Small**

Begin with one problem-solving lesson per week

Use "low-floor, high-ceiling" problems accessible to all students

- **Focus on Process**

Assess and give credit for reasoning, not just correct answers

Celebrate multiple solution strategies

- **Create a Supportive Environment**

Establish classroom norms that value risk-taking

Encourage collaboration and discussion

**Reminders for FUTURE Teachers:**

- **Problem-solving is a skill that can be taught**. It is not just for "smart" students.

- **The process is as important as the answer**. Always focus on how students think, not just what they produce.

- **Your role is to facilitate thinking**. It is best to ask good questions and create a safe environment for learners to explore.

- **Start where your students are**. Use appropriate problems and provide necessary scaffolding

- **Be a problem-solver yourself**. The best way to teach problem-solving is to model it.

**What are Heuristics?**

Heuristics are general strategies or "rules of thumb" for solving problems. They are not guaranteed algorithms but rather flexible approaches that often lead to solutions. Heuristics are like tools in a toolbox. You select the right tool for the specific job.

**Why Multiple Strategies?**

- Different problems require different approaches

- Students have diverse thinking styles

- Real-world problems rarely have single solution paths

- Develops mathematical flexibility and creativity

To be effective investigators, we need a toolkit of strategies.

**Looking for Patterns:** Numerical, geometric, visual.

**Organizing Data:** Using tables, charts, lists, and diagrams.

**Creating Representations:** Drawing pictures, graphs, or using physical manipulatives.

**Working Backwards:** Starting from a desired result.

**Considering Special Cases:** Testing with simple or extreme examples.

**Generalizing:** Moving from specific observations to a general rule.

**Conjecturing:** Stating a "I think that..." proposition clearly.

**NINE PROBLEM-SOLVING HEURISTICS**

**2.1 ALGEBRAIC APPROACH**

Using algebraic symbols and equations to represent and solve problems.

When to use an algebraic approach?

- Problems with unknown quantities

- Situations with clear relationships between variables

- When precise numerical answers are needed

**Example**** 1****:** A number plus twice that number is 45. Find the number.

**Solution:**

Let x = the number

Equation: x + 2x = 45

3x = 45

x = 15

**Example**** 2****:** Find three consecutive integers whose sum is 87.

**Solution:**

Let x = first integer, x+1 = second, x+2 = third

Equation: x + (x+1) + (x+2) = 87

Solve: 3x + 3 = 87 → 3x = 84 → x = 28

Answer: 28, 29, 30

**2.2 MODEL APPROACH**

Using visual representations like bar models, diagrams, or physical manipulatives.

When to use the model approach?

- Problems with part-whole relationships

- When students struggle with abstract thinking

- Ratio and proportion problems

**Example:** John has 3 times as many marbles as Peter. Together they have 48 marbles. How many does each have?

|  |  |  |
| --- | --- | --- |

John (3 units)

|  |
| --- |

Peter (1 units)

Total: 4 units = 48 marbles

1 unit = 12 marbles (Peter)

3 units = 36 marbles (John)

**2.3 MAKE A TABLE**

Organizing data systematically in a table format to identify patterns.

When to use this approach?

- Problems with multiple variables

- Looking for patterns

- When trial and error is needed

**Example:** How many different outfits can you make with 3 shirts and 2 pants?

**Solution:**

| **Shirt** | **Pant 1** | **Pant 2** |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Red** | Outfit 1 | Outfit 2 |
| **Blue** | Outfit 3 | Outfit 4 |
| **Green** | Outfit 5 | Outfit 6 |

**Answer:** 6 different outfits

**2.4 LISTING**

Systematically listing all possibilities to ensure completeness.

When to use this approach?

- Combinatorial problems

- When order or arrangement matters

- Probability problems

**Example:** List all 2-digit numbers you can make with digits 2, 4, 6 without repetition.

**Solution:** 24, 26, 42, 46, 62, 64

**2.5 LOOKING FOR PATTERNS**

Identifying and extending numerical or geometric patterns.

When to use this approach?

- Sequence problems

- Problems with repeated operations

- When a rule or formula can be generalized

**Example:** What is the 10th term in the sequence: 2, 5, 8, 11, ...?

**Pattern Recognition:**

Terms increase by 3 each time

Pattern start at 2, add 3 each time

nth term = 3n - 1

10th term = 3(10) - 1 = 29

**2.6 GUESS AND CHECK**

Making educated guesses and refining based on results.

When to use the guess and check approach?

- When other methods seem too complex

- Problems with limited possible answers

- To build intuition before formalizing

**Example:** I'm thinking of two numbers. Their product is 48, and their difference is 2. What are the numbers?

**Guessing Process:**

Try 8 and 6: product 48 ✓, difference 2 ✓

Answer: 8 and 6

**2.7 WORKING BACKWARDS**

Starting from the result and reversing the operations.

When to use working backward as an approach?

- Problems with a known end point

- When operations are reversible

- Age problems

**Example:** I did some shopping. I spent half of my money, then ₱100 more. I have ₱200 left. How much did I start with?

**Working Backwards:**

Before last spending: ₱200 + ₱100 = ₱300

This was half of original: Original = ₱300 × 2 = ₱600

**2.8 GEOMETRIC APPROACH**

Using geometric principles, diagrams, or spatial reasoning.

When to use the geometric approach?

- Problems involving shapes, space, or measurement

- Optimization problems

- When visualization helps understanding

**Example:** What's the area of a walkway 2m wide around a rectangular pool 20m × 15m?

**Geometric Solution:**

Outer rectangle: 24m × 19m (adding 2m each side)

Area = (24×19) - (20×15) = 456 - 300 = 156 m²

**Teaching Multiple Approaches**

**Example: **The Handshake Problem
*If 10 people each shake hands with everyone **else,** how many handshakes occur?*

**Approach 1: Listing/Systematic Counting**

Person 1 shakes with 9 others

Person 2 shakes with 8 others (already counted with person 1)

Each pair shakes hands once

Represent people as points, handshakes as lines

**3.1 INTRODUCTION TO MATHEMATICAL INVESTIGATION AND MODELLING**

**Introduction**

A mathematical investigation begins with a situation that must be understood or a set of data that must be organized and explained in mathematical terms. It is essential to run the risk of proposing conjectures. Testing these conjectures and collecting more data may support them or lead to new conjectures.

**What is Mathematical Investigation?**

A mathematical investigation allows students to examine situations using various techniques and, in the process of their exploration, develop skills that can be applied to other problems.

***Mathematical ******i******nvestigation***** **refers to a systematic exploration of mathematical situations to discover patterns, relationships, and properties, while ***m******athematical ******m******odeling***** **refers to the process of using mathematics to represent, analyze, and solve real-world problems

| **Mathematical Investigation** | **Mathematical Modelling** |
| --- | --- |
| Focuses on pure mathematics | Connects math to the real world |
| Explores patterns and relationships | Solves practical problems |
| Often open-ended | Usually has a specific application |
| Process-oriented | Solution-oriented |

**Example :** Investigate the relationship between the number of sides of a regular polygon and the number of  diagonals.

**Why conduct a Mathematics Investigation?**

Conduct a Mathematical Investigation for students to:

- Experience methods of planning, organizing, analyzing, and evaluating data;

- Freely choose what aspects of the problem situation they would like to pursue and what strategies they would use;

-  Apply appropriate mathematics or discover a mathematical relationship;

- Develop questions, approaches, and results that are at least for them, original products;

- Use the same general methods used by research mathematicians. They work through cycles of data gathering, visualization, abstraction, conjecturing, and proof;

- Communicate mathematically by describing their thinking, writing definitions and conjectures, using symbols, justifying their conclusions, and reading mathematics.

- To formulate their own questions from a given situation formulating their own questions, students give teachers a clear indication of their level of knowledge and/or understanding of the topic.

**Possible Approaches:**

- Draw polygons and count diagonals

- Look for patterns in the numbers

- Develop a general formula

- Test and verify the formula

- **Testing:** Check conjectures with new examples

- **Explaining:** Develop logical arguments

- **Communicating:** Present findings clearly

- **Extending:** Pose new related questions

**3.3 MATHEMATICAL PROBLEM POSING**

The art of creating new mathematical problems or reformulating existing ones.

**Problem Posing Strategies:**

**Strategy 1: ****What-if-not**

Change the conditions of existing problems

**Original Problem:** Find the area of a rectangle with length 8 and width 5

**What-If-Not Questions:**

- What if it's not a rectangle but a parallelogram?

- What if we fix the perimeter instead of dimensions?

- What if we want maximum area with fixed perimeter?

**Strategy 2: Generalize/Specialize**

*Making problems broader or more specific*

**Original:** Solve x² - 5x + 6 = 0
**Generalize:** For what values of k does x² - 5x + k = 0 have real roots?
**Specialize:** Find a quadratic with roots 2 and 3

**Strategy 3: Reverse Thinking**

*Starting from solution and working backward*

**Original:** Prove the Pythagorean theorem
**Reverse:** Given a² + b² = c², what can you say about the triangle?

**Strategy 4: Change Representation**

*Expressing the same concept differently*

**Algebraic → Geometric:** Show geometrically why (a+b)² = a² + 2ab + b²
**Numerical → Graphical:** Graph the relationship between roots and coefficients

**4.5 FORMULATING CONJECTURES**

**Developing Testable Hypotheses:**

- Based on observed patterns

- Clear and specific statements

- Mathematically precise

**Example:** For rectangles with fixed perimeter, the area is maximized when the rectangle is a square

**4.6 VERIFYING CONJECTURES**

**Testing with Multiple Cases:**

- Try various examples

- Look for counterexamples

- Consider edge cases

**Example:** Test the rectangle conjecture with perimeters 12, 16, 20, etc.

**4.7 JUSTIFYING CONJECTURE**

**Developing Mathematical Proofs:**

- Logical reasoning

- Algebraic justification

- Geometric arguments

**Example Rectangle Justification:**

- Let perimeter P = 2(l + w), so w = P/2 - l

- Area A = l × w = l (P/2 - l) = (P/2)l - l²

- This is quadratic, maximum at l = P/4, so w = P/4 → square

**4.8 WRITING THE SUMMARY AND THE POSSIBLE EXTENSIONS**

**Structuring the Investigation Report:**

- Introduction and problem statement

- Methodology and data collection

- Findings and conjectures

- Justification and proofs

- Conclusions and reflections

- Extensions and new questions

**Example Extensions:** “What about 3D shapes? What if we fix surface area instead of perimeter?"

**4.9 FINALIZING THE PAPER**

**Polishing the Investigation:**

- Clear and logical organization

- Proper mathematical notation

- Appropriate visuals and diagrams

- Correct grammar and spelling

**Written Report Guide (Approx. 3-5 pages)**

- **Title ****&**** Introduction:** State your investigation question and its origins.

- **The Process:** Detail your exploration. What strategies did you use? Include your tables, charts, and failed attempts! This is key.

- **Findings ****&**** Conjectures:** Clearly state the patterns you found and the conjectures you made.

- **Justification ****&**** Proof:** Provide your reasoning for why your conjectures are true. This can be a logical argument, a visual proof, or an algebraic proof.

- **Conclusions ****&**** Reflections:** Summarize your main discovery. What was the most challenging part? What new questions did your investigation raise?

- **Extensions:** Suggest one or two ways this investigation could be taken further.
